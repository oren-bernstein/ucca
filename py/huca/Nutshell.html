UCCA in a Nutshell

Version: 1.0

Date: 15/11/12

  1. UCCA encodes syntactic structure using conceptual cognitively-motivated terms.
  2. UCCA views the text as a sequence of scenes containing relations and participants.
  3. UCCA divides the text into units (not necessarily contiguous stretches of text), each referring to a relation, a participant in a relation or a relation along with its participants. The types of relations we annotate are listed below.
  4. The units must cover all the text.
  5. Units may contain other sub-units, giving rise to a tree structure.
  6. Each unit is assigned a category, reflecting its role in a super-ordinate relation in which it participates. The category does not necessarily reflect the internal organization of the unit.
  1. For instance, all the units in boldface have the same category, as they all describe “horse” in finer detail:
  1. “A beautiful horse”
  2. “A police horse”
  3. “A horse with no name”
  4. “The horse that won the race”
  5. “A winning horse”
  1. A single interpretation is fixed and the annotation proceeds accordingly. UCCA does not annotate ambiguity. Not in the word level, not in the phrase level, nor in the passage level.
  2. The text is segmented to a sequence of scenes; each can be conceptualized as a single mental image. Each scene is considered a unit. There is no category scene, rather the category of the scene unit reflects the role of that unit in super-ordinate relation it participates in. See below for a classification of scenes.
  1. “Woody walked in the park” (1 scene)
  2. “I got home and took a shower” (2 scenes)
  1. Each scene contains (one or more) participants (A). These are the principal participants in the relation referred to by the scene (including locations). Participants may refer either to physical or abstract entities.
  1. “[John]A boiled [an egg]A”
  2. “[Programming]A is widely taught nowadays”
  1. Scenes can either describe a state (a relation constant in time or a process) or a dynamic relation (one that evolves in time).
  2. In static scenes, the main relation is annotated as a state (S). The state includes its auxiliaries and modals. See more cases of states in Appendix D.
  1. “John [is tall]S”
  2. “John [could have been tall]S”
  3. “[The apple tree]A [is in]S [the garden]A”
  4. “[An apple]A [weighs]S [200g]A”
  1. In dynamic scenes, the main relation is marked as a process (P). The process includes its auxiliaries and modals:
  1. “John [kicked]P the ball”
  2.  “John [should kick]P the ball”
  1. Secondary verbs are verbs that semantically modify an existing verb, and do not refer to any specific activity or state. Secondary verbs should be included in the process or state they modify.
  1. “John [wants to kick]P the ball”
  2. “John [finished kicking]P the ball an hour ago”
  3. “John [seems to have kicked]P the ball”
  4. “John [seems to be tall]S”
  1. Secondary relations within the scene, referring to the entire scene (and not to one of the scene elements in particular), should be marked as adverbials (D)[1]. See Appendix C for how distinguish Ds and As in marginal cases.
  1. “His workers treat him [with disrespect]D”
  2. “John [cleverly]D answered the manager’s question”
  1. Role Markers (R) are also secondary relations that pertain to a specific participant and relate it to the scene. Rs are usually prepositions  (see Appendix C).
  1. “There are cookies [inR theE jarC]A”
  2. “They arrived [afterR dinnerC]D”
  3. If a word does not introduce any new relation and merely marks the role of the participant in existing relations, it is not an R (but an F, see below).
  1. “Children often rely [onF their parents]A”
  1. Complex units are units that have meaningful sub-parts (sub-units). We distinguish between two cases:
  1. The complex unit contains one or more relations (or properties) applied to a single argument. In this case the units corresponding to that argument are marked as centers (C) and the rest are marked as elaborators (E).
  1. “[Big]E [brown]E [dogs]C”
  2. “[chocolate]E [cookies]C”
  1. If it is hard to say which is the relation and which is its argument, both units should be marked as Cs.
  1. “[army]C [of]F [zombies]C” (example of two Cs)
  1. The entity in question contains a relation between two or more entities (usually coordination). In this case, we mark the relation as connector[2] (N) and each of the related entities as centers (C).
  1. “[[John]C [and]N [Mary]C]A went to school together”
  2. “[I]A [‘ll have]P  [[gin]C [and]N [tonic]C ]A”
  1. Some words do not refer to a participant or relation and can be correctly interpreted only in the context of a larger construction. Usually in these cases, they cannot be substituted with any other word. Such units are marked as function (F).
  1. Note that since F does not refer to a participant or relation and since UCCA’s hierarchy reflects participation in a relation, it is often not clear in exactly what level of the hierarchy an F is placed. When this occurs, try including the F in the deepest unit that stands to reason.
  2. Examples:
  1. “Would you like a [piece [of]F cake]A?”
  2. “John referred [[to]F [Mary]C]A”
  1. When some relation (corresponding to a unit X) is clearly described by the text, but either it or one of its arguments is not expressed explicitly in the text, we say the unit X is missing a sub-unit. If the missing entity is overtly referred to in another place in the text by the unit Y, we say that Y is a remote sub-unit of X. If the omitted unit does not appear explicitly in any place in the text, we say X has an implicit unit. Remote and Implicit have categories just like any other unit.
  1. Add a remote unit whenever you think there is a participant or relation which is strongly present in your conceptualization of the scene, but is not explicitly mentioned.
  2. Examples: (target relations (X) underlined, remote units (Y) boldfaced)
  1. “[[John]A got home]LU [and]L [took a shower]LU,REMOTE-A(“John”)”
  2. “[[The]E [dog]C [I saw last night]E,REMOTE-A(“dog”) ]A [was brown]S”
  3. “[We just opened]REMOTE-A(IMPLICIT)” (what was opened is implicit)
  4. “[ [John]A [[is]F [tall]C ]S]LU, [ [Mary]A [ [is]F [n’t]E ]S, REMOTE-C(“tall”) ]LU”
  5. “John [[wants]E ]P,REMOTE-C(IMPLICIT) food” (main verb omitted)
  1. Scenes may relate to one another in various ways (e.g., logical, temporal, contrastive). Such relations are marked linkers (L). Each of the linked scenes receives the category linked unit (LU).
  1. There are several special types of links. See Appendix A for an elaborate description of the relations between scenes.
  2. Linkers do not necessarily appear between the scenes they are linking (see example #2).
  3. We also allow linkers with a single argument. This usually happens if a linker relates one scene with everything that follows/precedes it, without there being any particular unit that the scene relates to  (see third example). Another case where we use a single argument linker is when one of its arguments is omitted.
  4. Examples:
  1. “[John managed to amuse himself]LU, [while]L [waiting in line]LU”
  2. “[If]L [you build it]LU, [they will come]LU”
  3. “[I’d done some research]LU , [asked a couple of questions]LU [and]L [found myself thinking.]LU “
  1. The term Ground in UCCA refers to the speech event, along with its speaker, hearer and/or their opinions of what’s described in the text. A unit is marked as a Ground Relation (G) (or Ground for short), if its primary purpose is to relate some unit to its ground. We place the G inside the unit it refers to.
  1. “[[Surprisingly]G, [our flight]A [arrived]P [on time]D]LU”
  2. “[[it is true]G [that]F [John]A [is coming]P [home]A ]LU”
  3. Note that if the ground evokes a complete scene (with internal structure), we analyze it as a scene, and not as a G:
  1. “[[I]A [was surprised]S ]LU [when]L [[our flight]A [arrived]P [on time]D]LU”
  2. "[I]A [told]P [you]A [already]D [that John can't make it ]A"

Technical Notes and Guidelines

  1. Note: Since morphology in English is very impoverished, we take a pragmatic approach and in our primary layer do not annotate parts of words, but only sets of complete words. In further layers, a sub-unit may cover a part of a word, as long as that part refers to a relation covered by UCCA or to a participant in it.
  2. When annotating a remote unit, select the largest possible relevant unit, and not its sub-units.
  3. Prefer Ds over Es inside sates or processes, when possible.
  4. Prefer participant scenes and elaborator scenes over linkage. That is, when it is possible to mark a scene as an elaborator or a participant scene, do so.
  5. Do not create units only to be used later as a remote unit. Use existing units instead.

Appendix A: Classification of Scenes

There are four major types of relations in which scenes may participate, and therefore four major types of categories they may receive:

  1. Participant scenes: Such scenes act as abstract participants and fill a slot in a larger scene.
  1. “[Talking to strangers]A [is ill-advised]S”
  2. “[[John]A [’s]F [accurate]D [kick]P]A [saved]P [[the]E [game]C]A”
  3. “[John]A [said]P [he’s coming home]A“
  1. Elaborator scenes: scenes that serve as elaborators of another unit. In that case, the scene is an E and has a remote A, which is the elaborated unit.
  1. “[The]E [dog]C [ [I]A [saw]P [last night]D ]E,REMOTE-A(“dog”)”
  2. “[The]E [person]C [ [whom]F [I]A [gave]P [the present]A [to]F]E,REMOTE-A(“person”)”
  1. Linked scenes (including Adverbial scenes): scenes that are linked to one another by some linker. In that case they should receive the category linked unit. In UCCA, scenes that act as adverbials in another scene are also considered linked scenes and not an adverbial within the other scene.
  1. “[You’re only saying this]LU [because]L [John told you to]LU”
  2. “[The minute]L [I got home]LU, [I noticed the new painting]LU”
  1. Autonomous scenes: scenes that are not related to any other units and are therefore in the top level of organization in the text. In these cases, we also mark them as LUs.

Note: Note that a scene is not necessarily something that can stand on its own. It may necessitate a larger construction to rely on, but it is still considered a scene:

  1. “[he retired]LU [with]L [a rank of major]LU”
  2. “[once poor]LU, [he now owns a 10-room apartment]LU”

Appendix B: Classification of Prepositional Phrases

There are three main varieties of prepositional phrases.

  1. Prepositions with significant semantic input: prepositions that add some semantic input on their own. They should be marked as Rs:
  1. “There are cookies [inR theE jarC]A”
  2. “They arrived [afterR dinnerC]A”
  3. “John put his hat [onR theE  shelfC]A.”
  1. Constructional Prepositions: in cases where the preposition has no substantial semantic input, the preposition should be marked as F. The prepositions in this case are a part of the construction or pattern they appear in and do not have a significant semantic input on their own.
  1. Examples: (constructional prepositions in boldface)
  1. “Let’s discuss this later [inF moreE detailC]D”
  2. “[Woody]A treated [him]A [withF disrespectC]D”
  1. Inherent prepositions: a common case of constructional prepositions occurs when a verb must appear with a certain preposition, without resulting in a change of meaning. Note that in this case, the F is in the scene-level and not in the argument’s level.
  1. “John relies [on]F [Mary’s sense of decency]A”
  2. “John referred [to]F [Aristotle]A in his thesis”
  1. Phrasal verb: the preposition changes the semantics of the verb in an unpredictable way. In that case the preposition is considered to be a part of the state or process. The state or process along with the preposition is an unanalyzable unit (as it does not have sub-parts with significant semantic input).
  1. “John [gave up]P his pension”
  2. “John [let]P Mary [down]P(CONTINUED)”
  3.  “John [took]P Mary [up on]P(CONTINUED) her promise”

Appendix C: Participant-Adverbial Distinction

A basic issue in almost any syntactic theory is to determine when a unit is a participant and when it is a secondary relation. In UCCA, this is the distinction between participants (As) and adverbials (Ds).

As are participants in the scene, and therefore the conceptualization of the scene requires them. They may either be referred to explicitly or implicitly.

Adverbials are secondary relations in the scene and therefore supply additional information on top of the main relation described in the scene. They are therefore not required for the conceptualization of the scene.

In a nutshell:

  1. Any unit that introduces a participant  and not a relation is an A. Subjects, objects, instruments, locations, destinations are therefore invariably As.
  2. Adverbs and any unit that introduces another, secondary, relation in the scene is a D. Manner adverbs (e.g., ‘quickly’, ‘politely’) are invariably Ds.
  3. By convention, we invariably mark temporal relations as Ds (they will be given a separate category in subsequent layers of UCCA).

Prepositional phrases constitute most of the borderline cases. Examples:

  1. “[John]A cut [the cake]A [withR aE knifeC]A”
  2. “[John]A took [one]A [forR theE teamC]A”
  3. “[John]A behaved [recklessly]D”
  4. “[Woody]A walked [inR theE parkC]A [yesterday]D”
  5. “[Woody]A treated [him]A [withF disrespectC]D”

Appendix D: Other Constructions

Scenes.

  1. Distinguishing Ground and Participant Scenes: A ground relates the speech event or some aspect of it with a given unit. It does not introduce a new scene above and beyond evoking the speech event.
  1. Examples: (ground)
  1. “[[The truth is]G [that]F [John]A [is]S [a conservative]A]LU”
  2. “[[Surprisingly]G [I]A [saw]P [John]A [in the park]A]LU”
  3. “[[To my surprise]G [I]A [saw]P [John]A [in the park]A]LU”
  1. Contrast with participant scenes. Both “I think” and “Mary saw” introduce a new scene, with a new P. They are therefore participant scenes.
  1. “[I]A [think]P [that]F [John is a conservative]A” 
  2. “[Mary]A [saw]P [John running in the park]A”
  1. Static Scenes. Static scenes are scenes which can be fully described by a single picture, no temporal dimension involved. Following are several examples of static scenes.
  1. Identity. Expressing the identity between two entities[3]:
  1. “[The morning star]A [is]S [the evening star]A”
  1. Attribution/Benefaction/Location. Specifying a quality, a benefactor or a location of an argument.
  1. “[This man]A [is clever]S”
  2. “[This present]A [is for]S [John’s birthday]A”
  3. “[The apple tree]A [is in]S [the garden]A”
  1. Possession.
  1. “[This book]A [is]S- [John]A [’s]-S”
  1. Existential There. This is a special type of a static scene. Since “there are” determines the relation here (which is existence), they are marked as the state.
  1. “[There are]S [earrings]A [onR theE tableC]A”
  1. Note that, as always, the category is not defined by the words comprising the unit, but by the function it has in the given scene. Consider these pairs of examples:
  1. “[John]A [is sitting]P [[in]Rthe garden]A” / “[The apple tree]A [is in]S [the garden]A”
  2. “[John]A [bought]P [wine]A [forR MaryE ’sF birthdayC]A” / “[This present]A [is for]S [John’s birthday]A”
  1. One scene or two. This amounts to the question of whether the two verbs describe relations that are similar in their participants, in their time and location and in their ground or whether the opposite is true. In some cases, two relations are fused together to describe one complex relation.
  1. Examples: (scenes underlined)
  1. “I got home and took a shower” (2 scenes)
  2. “you can go home, for all I care” (2 scenes)
  3. “it took a lot of effort to win this fight” (2 scenes)
  4. “he is on vacation, sailing a yacht near Greece” (2 scenes)
  5. “John eats and drinks enthusiastically” (1 scene)
  6. “He yelled and swore to no avail” (1 scene)
  7. “He is planning to kick the ball” (2 scenes) (“to kick the ball” is a participant scene of “he is planning”)
  8. “She went away angry” (1 scene, the two original scenes, of her going away and of her being angry, are fused into one)
  1. Secondary Verb or Participant Scene. Distinguishing between secondary verb constructions and participant scene constructions is done by determining whether the sentence in question refers to one or two scenes. Participant Scenes correspond to cases where there are two separate scenes, while secondary verbs correspond to the cases where there are two verbs, one dependent (secondary, not describing a scene in its own right, could not by itself be the P/S of a scene) and one independent (the main verb) within the same scene.
  1. Examples:
  1. “[He]A [was planning]P [to kick the ball]A,REMOTE-A(“He”) [for a long time]D” (two scenes)
  2. “[He]]A [demanded]P [to see the manager]A,REMOTE-A(“He”)]” (two scenes)
  3. “[He]A [wanted to kick]P [the ball]A” (one scene)
  1. Scenes with neither a P nor an S. Some scenes have no process nor state, since it is omitted or implied. In this case, we should add them as remote units.
  1. “[John bought eggs]LU [and]L [[Mary]A [chewing gum]A]LU,REMOTE-P(“bought”)”
  2. “[John wanted a real life]LU, [[not]D [life in a caravan]A]LU,REMOTE-A(“John”),REMOTE-P(“wanted”)”
  1. Modification by Ds and by Es. In some cases, an argument contains an entire scene. In that case, the argument is construed as one entity for the purposes of the super-ordinate relation, but may also have the internal structure of a scene. In that case, we analyze it internally as a scene. We should distinguish two types of modifying units:
  1. Adverbials refer to the scene itself and express a secondary relation in that scene:
  1. “[[John]A [’s]F [accurate]D [kick]P]A [saved]P [the game]A”
  1. Elaborators refer to scene when it is construed as a single entity:
  1. “[The]E [final]E [countdown]C”
  1. In some cases, we have both elaborators and adverbials in a single unit. In that case, the scene (including all its adverbials) should be marked as a C (with the internal structure of a scene), and the elaborators as Es:
  1. “[[The]E [[accurate]D [kick]P]C ]A saved the game”
  2. “[ [[Ali]E [‘s]F ]C [final]E [ [fight]P]C(CONT.) ]A will surely be remembered”
  1. Quantity Adverbs. Adverbs of quantity such as “just” and “only” should preferably be annotated as Ds when possible.
  1. “[There is]S [only]D [one piece of cake]A”
  2. “[The supermarket] [is]S(CONT.) [just]D [around]S(CONT.) [the corner]A”
  1. Fragments. Occasionally, a fragment of text does not describe a scene in its own right, nor belongs to any other specific scene. The category of such a unit, as always, is determined by its role in a super-ordinate relation it participates in (if any).
  1. “[No]LU, [this will not stand]LU”
  2. “[Darkness there]LU [and]L [nothing more]LU”
  1. Two Types of verbs that take a participant scene: Note that some verbs with a participant scene have a remote unit taken from the participant scene or vice versa. Other verbs do not exhibit such behavior.
  1. “[[I]A [expected]P [John to come]A]LU”
  2. “[[We]A [agreed]P [for John to give the funeral oration]A]LU”
  3. “[I persuaded [John to come]A]LU,REMOTE-A(”JOHN”)”
  4. “[John promised [to be better]A,REMOTE-A(”JOHN”)]LU”
  1. Raising with Secondary Relations: A relation referring to the entire scene is mentioned in addition to the main relation of the scene. It is analyzed as an E+C construction.
  1. “[John]A [ [is]F [easy]E [to]F [please]C ]P”
  2. “[John]A [ [is]F [likely]E [to]F [leave]C ]P”
  3. Similar but not quite the same: “[War]A [is likely]P”  (it’s a P and not an S, since it is “likely to break” which is a dynamic relation in nature)

Participants.

  1. Prominent Cases of Remote Participants. A remote participant is a unit that is referenced in a scene in which it is not contained. By convention, the remote participant should be chosen as the minimal unit that refers to the target entity (for instance, “table” and not “the red table”).
  1. Several prominent cases of remote participants: (target scenes underlined; remote participants boldfaced)
  1. Coordination. The subject is often omitted.
  1. “John had dinner and went to bed”
  1. Relative scenes. The head of the elaboration is not contained in the relative scene.
  1. “[The table I’m using]A [[is]F [too]E [short]C]S”
  1. Infinitives. The subject is omitted.
  1. “Driving to school upsets John”
  2. “to be expected to wash the car infuriates Mary”

Nominals.

  1. Possession. Possession constructions (by noun compounding, by “’s” or by “of”) is used to express several different relations. Typically, determining the head in such cases is straightforward. There are several important special cases: “Corresponding Profiles” and “Part-Whole Relations” (see below).
  1. “[This]E [seat]C [of]F [the Knesset]E”
  2. “[John]E [’s]F [car]C”
  1. Determiners. Determiners should be annotated as elaborators of the noun.
  1. “[The]E [Knesset]C”
  2. “[A]E [big]E [brown]E [dog]C”
  1. Corresponding Profiles. The case where two units referring to the same entity form together an entity for the purposes of a super-ordinate relation (say, form an A) and that it is not clear which elaborates which. In this case, each of them is considered center[4].
  1. “[[an]E [army]C [of]F [zombies]C ]”
  2. “[his strange belief]C [that]F [chickens are immortal]C”
  3. “[the rumor]C [that]F [John kicked the ball]C”
  1. Extraposition. Cases where an elaborator does not create a contiguous stretch of text with its center. In this case, they should be marked together as a non-contiguous unit.
  1. “He saw [that painting]A before, [[that lovely magnificent painting]E]A(CONT)”
  2. “I met [the guy]A yesterday, [[whom I first saw in the park]E]A(CONT)”
  1. Fused Relative scenes.  There are many constructions that resemble a relative scene construction, but have a somewhat different form. Their internal structure should look like that of a scene:
  1. “[[What]A [I]A [meant]P]A [was]S [I want to have dinner]A”
  2. “[ [Any]E [recipes]C [[she]A [used]P]E-REMOTE-A(“receipes”) ]A [are marked]P [in red]D”
  1. Nominalization. The process by which a scene is converted into an abstract participant. Nominalizations should be internally analyzed as scenes, with a P/S, As, Ds and Rs. However, nominalizations may also be modified by nominal elaborators. In that case, they should be marked as Es.
  1. “[Him destroying the city]A [[was]F [a disaster]C ]S”
  2. “[ [His]A [destruction]P [of]F [the city]A ]A [[was]F [a disaster]C ]S”
  3. “[ [The]E [tedious]E [ [negotiations]P [between]F [John]A [and]F [Mary]A ]C ]A [lasted]P [3 years]A”
  1. Numbers and Quantifiers. They are considered elaborators. The question of their scope is not addressed in the current layer of the annotation. Therefore they are considered a part of the unit adjacent to it.
  1. “[[All]E [Greeks]C]A are mortals”
  2. “[[Two]E [bananas]C]A are lying on the table”
  3. “[[Millions]E [of]F [homes]C]”

This also holds for quantities. Quantities are usually comprised of a magnitude and a unit’s name (e.g., 100m or 3,000 dollars). They should be annotated as a E+C.

  1. “I’m going to give him [100E USDC]A for his job”
  1. Noun Phrase Structure. The approach taken in this primary layer of UCCA to NP structure is a minimalist one. We do not attempt to represent the inter-relations between the elaborators. Instead, where there are several elaborators of the same entity, we annotate them as a flat structure with Es and a C.
  1. “[the]E [big]E [brown]E [dog]C”
  2. “[Greek]E [cucumber]E [salad]C”
  3. “[a]E [loud]E [student]E [assembly]C”[5]
  1. Nominal Predicate. Occasionally nominals are used as predicates. They are accompanied by a copula. UCCA takes the following approach: If the nominal invokes an object or participant, we annotate it as an A. If the nominal expresses a relation (usually the case when the nominal refers to a relation), we mark it as a P or S.
  1. “[China]A [is]S [the greatest place on earth]A”
  2. "[John]A [is]S [my history teacher]A"
  3. "[his speech]A [was an embarrassment]S"
  1. Classifiers. Units comprised of a sub-unit that specifies the whole W, and another which specifies which category it belongs to P (in order to specify what aspect of meaning we would like to refer to). In these cases, UCCA will annotate W as a C and P as an E.
  1. “[the name]E [John]C”
  2. “[the year]E [1966]C”
  3. “[the rumor]E [of]F [his retirement]C”
  1. Part-Whole relations. Units comprised of a sub-unit that specifies the whole W, and another that specifies the specific sub-part of it P. In these cases, UCCA will annotate W as a C and P as an E.
  1. “[bottom]E [of]F [the]E [sea]C”
  2. “[sea]C [bottom]E”
  3. “[a]E [period]E [of]F [time]C”
  1. Prepositions without a following NP. In some cases, prepositions are used to indicate an abstract location which is not explicitly mentioned in the text. In these cases, we annotate them as Ds, as they add more information to the scene without introducing another participant[6].
  1. “[come]P [in]D”
  2. “[dance]P [the night]A [away]D”

Processes/States.

  1. P/S Sub-units. If the process/state is multi-worded, it will usually contain sub-units. The main verb is generally the center; other sub-units that have significant semantic input, chiefly modals and secondary verbs, are elaborators. Auxiliary verbs (be and have), which do not have significant semantic input in their own right[7] are considered function.
  2. Modals. Should invariably be annotated as secondary verbs (and therefore as Es). This applies to “would” and “will” as well.
  1. “John [[will]E [come]C]P”
  2. “Mary [[should]E [come]C]P”
  1. Dislocated auxiliaries in questions. Should be annotated as a (non-contiguous) part of the process/state.
  1. “[Who]A [didn’t]P [you]A [invite]P(CONTINUED) ?
  1. Verbal Es and Adverbials. A common ambiguity is between Es inside a P/S and Ds. In case the unit refers directly to the verb and constitutes an inseparable part of it (as in secondary verbs), we mark it as an E inside the P/S. In any other case, we mark it as a D.
  1. “[I] [didn’t]S- [always]D [like]-S(CONT.) him”
  2. “[I]A [must eat]P”
  1. Non-contiguous Process/State. Occasionally, an adverbial is placed between the auxiliary and the main verb. In that case, a non-contiguous process/state should be marked.
  1. “[I]A [am]P [still]D [waiting]P(CONTINUED) [for]E [your reply]A”
  1. Infinitive “to”. By convention, when “to” is used as function, it should be included within the process/state.
  1. “He [wanted to come]P home”
  2. “[to [kick]P a penalty shot in soccer]A is often exciting”
  1. Light Verbs. The nominal denoting the action is considered to be a part of the process/state. There is no internal structure in the process/state.
  1. “[John]A [took a shower]P“
  2. “[Mary]A [gave]P [John]A [a smile]P(CONT)“
  1. Make X ADJ. In this construction, the adjective is a part of the process, and is in fact an E in the P.
  1. “[John]A [ [makes]C ]P [Mary]A [ [happy]E ]P(CONT.)”
  1. Secondary Verbs with another role. Some secondary verbs introduce another role beside the roles of the main verb. An example is “help”, “force” and “permit”. Like all secondary verbs, such verbs are considered an E inside the process/state. The additional participant is marked as an A in the scene.
  1. “[John]A [[helped]E]P [Mary]A [[climb]C]P(CONT.) [the ladder]A”
  2. “[John]A [ [forced]E]P [Mary]A [ [to]F [climb]C]P(CONT.) [the ladder]A”
  3. "[he]A [[is]F [guilty]E [of]F [not]E [cleaning]C]P [the dishes]A"
  1. Remember-Forget: These verbs can be used either as secondary verbs (applying to the same scene as the main verb) or as primary verbs (in that case, there are two separate scenes). This decision is context-dependent, and the annotation of these verbs depends on the decision whether to annotate a single scene or two scenes:
  1. “[John]A [ [remembered]E [to]F [take]C ]P [the keys]A“
  2. “[John]A [forgot]P [[how]E [to]F [ride]P [his bicycle]A]A”

Other Relations.

  1. Punctuation. Invariably considered function in the current layer of UCCA (even commas).
  2. WH-pronouns. WH-pronouns should be annotated with the same category as the participant they refer to. In some cases (notably relative clauses), the wh-pronoun does not refer to an entity, is merely a part of the construction and is therefore F.
  1. “[How]E could you do that?”
  2. “[Who]A shot the Sheriff?”
  3. “[[Which]E car]A did you buy?”
  4. “[the]E [man]C [[who]F wasn’t there]E,REMOTE-A(“MAN”)”
  1. Non-contiguous Linkers. In some cases, the linkers do not form one contiguous unit. We mark them by convention as two separate linkers and not as a non-contiguous unit. The units linked by these two linkers are the same.
  1. “[Either]L you buy it [or]L you don’t”
  1. Passive “by”. The “by” of the passive should be annotated as F.
  1. “He is considered [[by]F many]A to be the best chef town.”
  1. Dates and Names. Dates and names are treated as unanalyzable. Therefore, no sub-units should be annotated:
  1. “I live in [New York]A”
  2. “The event took place [[on]E [May 17th, 1832]C]D”
  1. Unanalyzable Expressions. It is not necessary to annotate the internal structure of every multi-word unit. If the unit does express any internal relations (that is, relations besides the relation it participates in), no sub-units should be annotated:
  1. “I did it [on my own]D”
  2. “[The British Empire]A was the largest empire in history”
  1. Complex Prepositions. Some prepositions are multi-worded. They should be marked as a unit with no sub-units.
  1. “[[According to]E [John]C]G, the soup is too salty”
  2. “[Mary]A [ [is]F [in charge]C [of]F]S [John]A”

Morphology.

  1. Inflectional and Derivational Morphology. UCCA does not annotate them in the current layer. Therefore the word “dogs” has no sub-units and neither does the word “talked”. This will be added in future layers.
  2. Coersed Word/Phrase. Several words that were coersed into one and obtained their own idiosyncratic meaning. In this layer of UCCA they should be analyzed as a single unit, without sub-units.
  1. “There are [pickpockets]A in this side of town”
  2. “[he]A [ [’s]F [a]E [have-been]C ]S”
  3. Let’s go again [[on]E [the]E [merry-go-round]C]E”

Appendix E: Plain Text Notation

In order to make UCCA’s annotation legible and standardized, we give here guidelines for UCCA’s notation in plain text.

We note that the hierarchical structure formed by UCCA can be annotated by standard bracketing. The abbreviation of the category should be either adjacent to the left or to the right side of the category.

For example, annotating the word “apple” with the category X should look like “[X apple]” or “[apple X]”.

We use the following abbreviations for the categories:

LU or U - linked unit

A - participant

C - center

L - linker

D - adverbial

E - elaborator

G - ground

S - state

N - connector

P - process

R - role marker

F - Function

Non-contiguity: We use a dash to indicate a continuation of a unit. For example, if "word1 … word2" is a non-contiguous unit then we mark it "[X- word1] [Y] [Z] [W] [-X word2]".

In case there are two non-contiguous units nested within one another, and of the same category, we may use indices to disambiguate. For example, in the sequence “w1 w2 w3 w4 w5”, if “w1 … w4” is a non-contiguous unit of category X and “w2...w5” is also a non-contiguous unit of category X, we mark it “[X1- w1] [X2- w2] w3 [-X2 w4] [-X1 w5]”.

Remote Units: To mark remote units, we first assign an index to the unit that appears as a remote unit. We mark that index immediately after the category (e.g., “[X1 w1]”). If w1 is then a remote unit in another unit, we mark it by adding the index inside curly brackets. For example, we can mark “[X1 w1] [Y{X1-CAT} w2]”, meaning that X1 is a remote unit in Y of category CAT. When the category of the original occurrence of the remote unit (X) is the same as the category it has in its remote occurrence, we can omit the specification of the category CAT and simply write “[X1 w1] [Y{X1} w2]”.

Implicit Units: Implicit units are marked much like remote units, but instead of writing a pointer to the remote unit, we write a fixed expression “IMP”. For instance: “[X1 w1] [Y{IMP-A} w2]”.

Linkage: The participants of a linkage are marked on the linker of that linkage[8]. As with remote units, we assign an index to the linkage arguments. If X and Y are of types C and D, we index them as, say, C1 and D1. If Z is a linker that links X and Y together, we mark it by appending ‘C1’ and ‘D1’ inside triangular brackets (‘>’ and ‘<’) to the category notation of Z, thusly: “[L<C1,D1> Z]”. If the linker (Z) has a single argument, it should appear alone inside the triangular brackets: “[L<C1> Z]”. For example:

  1. "[L1<LU1,LU2> When] [LU1 [A I] [P came] [A home]], [LU2 [A I] [P- took] [A my shoes] [-P off]]"
  2. “[Nevertheless L<LU1>], [this cannot be tolerated LU1]”

[1] There is sometimes ambiguity as to whether the adverbial refers to the entire scene or to the state/process. We generally do not resolve this ambiguity and both cases are considered adverbials.

[2] In rare cases, the relation may be remote or implicit (see below).

[3] Do not confuse identity with “Nominal Predicates” (see below).

[4] It is true that in a certain context, one of the centers may be considered more central than the other (the other being an elaboration). However, since this distinction is very fine, we refrain from annotating it explicitly and instead mark both as centers in such cases.

[5] In some cases, where one E clearly elaborates another E and not the C, we do annotate the internal structure. For example: “[ [heavy]E [water]C ]E [production]C”.

[6] One could say they introduce a very abstract location. However, since the location is very abstract its closer to a relation than to a participant, in our view.

[8] For simplicity, we assume linkers are contiguous units. If a linker has two non-contiguous parts (as in “either...or”), we mark it by convention as two separate linkers with the same arguments. See also “other relations” in Appendix D.

UCCA: Worked-out Examples

This document details the annotation of a single passage according to the primary layer of UCCA. It is accompanied with explanations for each of the cases. The general flow of annotation is as follows:

  1. Identify scenes.
  2. Identify the links between them.
  3. Identify the relations and participants in the scene.
  4. Analyze the complex units.

Note that all these examples can be found as annotated passages in the UCCA application.

Example #1:

“When your community manager joins you, do your best to ensure that she is able to get feedback from the community to key members of your organization. Community managers are always seen as primary contact points in communities, and you should expect them to receive input, opinion, concerns, worries, and other useful feedback from the community.” (“The Art of Community”/Jono Bacon, p. 356)

Delimiting Scenes:

We start with delimiting the text into scenes, and detecting their linkers. The delimitation into scenes imposes constraints on the further analysis of the utterance.

  1. [When L<LU1,LU2>]
  2. [your community manager joins you LU1],
  3. [do your best to ensure that she is able to get feedback from the community to key members of your organization LU2].
  4. [Community managers are always seen as primary contact points in communities LU3],
  5. [and L<LU3,LU4>]
  6. [you should expect them to receive input, opinion, concerns, worries, and other useful feedback from the community LU4]

The two cases that raise a difficulty here are whether to separate (c) and (f) into two separate scenes or not. In both cases we have a verb which has a complement clause and we should determine whether it is one scene (with a secondary verb), two scenes where one is a participant in the other or two linked scenes. In (c), this is a case of a secondary verb that introduces another role (see “Secondary verbs with another role” in the nutshell document). In (f), this is a case of a participant scene (see “Two Types of verbs that take a participant scene” in the nutshell).

We should also mark the arguments of the linkers. In our case it is clear that the “when” in (a), links the scene following it and the one following that, while the “and” in (g) links (f) and (h) between which it is situated.

Identifying Scene Elements:

We now mark the main scene elements: participants, processes, states and adverbials.

  1. [When L]
  2. [your community manager A] [joins P] [you A1],

“your community manager” and “you” are the main participants in this scene, whose main relation is “joins”. It is a process since the scene evolves in time (otherwise, it would have been a state). Note that we include all the elaborators of “manager” (i.e., “your” and “community”) inside the boundaries of the unit.

  1. [ [ [do your best E] [to F] [ensure C] E] P-] [that F] [she A] [ [is F] [able E] [to F] [get C] -P] [feedback A] [from the community A] [to key members of your organization A]. (A1 - remote A)

In this case, there is a complex P, which includes a secondary verb that introduces another role (“do your best to ensure”) and a main verb “is able to get”. The main participant here that should have filled the agent slot of “do your best to ensure” is omitted. It is “you”, which is explicitly mentioned in the previous scene (A1). By convention, we choose the most recent mention of the omitted participant to serve as the remote A.

Both the secondary verb “do your best to ensure” and “is able to get” have secondary verbs (“do your best” and “is able” respectively). They are internally analyzed accordingly.

Note that “that” is an F and not an L, since it does not refer to a new relation and is merely a part of a larger construction. In contrast, “when” and “and”, which we encountered earlier, do add semantic content of their own.

  1. [Community managers A] [are P-] [always D] [seen -P] [as F] [primary contact points in communities A],

In this case, we are first encountering an adverbial. It is “always”. Since it is intervening between the auxiliary and the main verb, the process here forms a discontiguous unit. In addition, we have two As. Note that “always” is a D. It could be said it is an E inside the P, but as we do not distinguish between the two in this layer, i mark it as a D (see also footnote #2 in “UCCA in a Nutshell”).

  1. [and L]
  2. [you A] [should expect P] [[them A] [to F] [receive P] [input, opinion, concerns, worries, and other useful feedback A] [from the community A]]

Here we have one scene acting as a participant in another scene. Note that “them” is not a participant in the previous scene (unlike in “you persuaded them to receive a present” where “them” is an A both in the main scene and the participant scene, because they are both persuaded and receive a present). The “to” is F since it does not introduce a new relation or participant.

Analyzing Complex Entities and Adding Referents:

  1. [ [your E] [community E] [manager C] A2] [joins P] [you A1]

Since “your community manager” has the same reference as “manager”, it is considered C. The other two elements have other references and add more information to the center and are therefore marked E.

  1. [ [do your best E] [to F] [ensure C] P-] [she A] [[is F] [able E] [to F] [get C] -P] [feedback A] [ [from R] [the E] [community C] A] [ [to R] [key E] [members C] [of F] [ [your E] [organization C] E] A].

“do your best” has no internal structure, since “your” is not substitutable. The “your” does not introduce a new entity, it merely has to agree with the subject of the sentence (which is in this case omitted).

The process’s center is “get” and it has a secondary verb “able”. There are two peripheral arguments (which are considered in UCCA As): “from the community” and “to key members of your organization”. The prepositions “from” and “to” are considered Rs, since they can be substituted (e.g., “to” can be substituted by “into” or “in”, “from” can be substituted by “out of” or “through”).

“she” refers to “your community manager” (we always annotate the last (non-pronoun, non-deictic) occurrence of that word).

  1. [[Community E] [managers C] A3] [are P-] [always  D] [seen -P] [[as F] [primary E] [contact E] [points C] [ [in R] [communities C] E] A]

“as” is a function, since it does not bring semantic input of its own above and beyond the semantics of “seen”. “in communities” is a complex elaborator. Inside of it, the “in” is an elaborator of “communities”, since it can be substituted by, e.g., “on”.

  1. [you A] [ [should E] [expect C] P] [[them A] [to F] [receive P] [ [input C], [opinion C], [concerns C], [worries C], [and N] [ [other E] [useful E] [feedback C] C2] A] [ [from E] [the E] [community C] A]

The structure of the object here is a coordination structure. Each of the conjuncts should be marked as an C, and the coordinating conjunction (“and” in this case) should be marked N.

“them” refers to “community managers”. Note that this entity also appears in earlier parts of the text, but we always seek its last occurrence (excluding pronouns and deictic expressions).

Example #2:

“Developers on every project come and go, and Ubuntu is no

different. When you leave or disengage from the project, in

whole or in part, we ask that you do so in a way that minimizes

disruption to the project. This means you should tell people

you are leaving and take the proper steps to ensure that others can pick up where you leave off.” (“The Art of Community” / Jono Bacon, p. 42)

Delimiting Scenes:

  1. [Developers on every project come and go LU1]

There is a question here of whether to separate it into two scenes or not. Since “come and go” both share participants and ultimately refer to the same action, we do not separate it.

  1. [and L<LU1,LU2>]
  2. [Ubuntu is no different LU2]
  3. [When L<LU3,LU4>]
  4. [you leave or disengage from the project, in whole or in part LU3]

Again, there are two coordinated verbs: “leave” and “disengage”. However, again they share participants and refer to the same action. We therefore view it as one scene.

  1. [we ask that you do so in a way that minimizes disruption to the project LU4]

Note that “when” links scenes LU3 and LU4.

  1. [This means L<LU4,LU5>]

“This means” does not introduce a scene and is merely used to link what was said previously to what is said from here on.

  1. [you should tell people you are leaving LU5]
  2. [and L<LU5,LU6>]
  3. [take the proper steps to ensure that others can pick up LU6]
  4. [where L<LU6,LU7>]
  5. [you leave off LU7]

Here the question of whether to separate it into two scenes or not comes up again. The question is whether they are viewed as one scene (with “take the proper steps to ensure” a secondary verb) or two scenes. Since “take the proper steps to ensure” ultimately does not describe an action or state in its own right, but is rather dependent on another verb, we see them as one scene. Note that the secondary verb here introduces a new role (filled by the remote A, “you”). This is similar to the case with “ensure” in Example 1.

Identifying Scene Elements:

  1. [Developers on every project A] [come and go P]

There is a question of whether “on every project” is a part of “developers” or whether it is a participant in its own right. It can go either way, but in my view the “on” is more linked to the development then to general location (which could have been the case had “in” been used).

  1. [and L]
  2. [Ubuntu A] [is S-] [no D] [different -S]

Note that negation is invariably handled in the current layer of UCCA as a D.

  1. [when L]
  2. [you A] [leave or disengage P] [from the project A] , [in whole or in part D]

“the project” is a participant. On the other hand, “in whole or in part” is a (secondary) relation in the scene: its semantics is separate from the P’s and it adds more information to it. It is therefore a D.

  1. [we A] [ask P] [that F] [ [you A] [ [do so in a way that F] [minimizes C] P] [disruption to the project A] A]

The form of this sentence is very misleading. On the face of it, we have a main scene with an elaborator which itself has an elaborator scene. On closer examination, we see that “do so in a way that” conveys very little new information. For instance, we could say instead “... we ask that you minimize disruption to the project”.

The expression is mostly used to add some politeness to it. We therefore mark it as an F. The fact that it has sub-parts which do correspond to some more specific meaning (‘do’ schematically refers to an action, ‘way’ to the manner in which it was done etc.), is not annotated in this layer since a sub-unit of an F in the current layer is not analyzable. Future layers will address politeness constructions.

  1. [This means L]

Although the linker has the internal structure of a scene, this is a very degenerated case. We therefore refrain from analyzing it internally as a scene.

  1. [you A] [should tell P] [people A] [ [you A2] [are leaving P] A]
  2. [and L]
  3. [ [ [take the proper steps E] [to F] [ensure C] E] P-] [that F] [others A] [ [can E] [pick up C] -P]

In this scene we have a remote A which is “you” (A2). It is omitted since in a coordination of two scenes with a shared subject, the second subject may be omitted.

We also have a secondary verb, namely “take the proper steps”.

“pick up” is a phrasal verb, in the sense that it has a different meaning from the verb “pick”. We therefore include “up” as part of the process.

  1. [where L]
  2. [you A] [leave off P]

The same goes for “leave off”, which has a different meaning from “leave”.

Analyzing Complex Entities and Adding Referents:

  1. [ [Developers C] [[on E] [every E] [project C] E] A] [ [come C] [and N] [go C] ]

“on every project” has a C “project” and two Es - “on” and “every”. “on” is an E and not an F since it brings its own semantic content. “come and go” is a coordination. It has two conjuncts, marked as Cs - “come” and “go” and a connector “and”[1].

  1. [and L]
  2. [Ubuntu A] [ [is F] S-] [no D] [different C] -S]

“is” is an auxiliary and therefore F. Negation is considered a D in the current layer of UCCA.

  1. [when L]
  2. [you A] [ [leave C] [or N] [disengage C] P] [ [from R] [the E] [project C] A] , [ [[in F] [whole C] C] [or N] [ [in F] [part C] C] D]

“in whole” and “in part” are two coordinated elaborators, linked by “or”. Internally, they contain constructional prepositions (see Appendix B) “in” and centers “whole” and “part”.

  1. [we A] [ask ] [that F] [ [you A] [ [do so in a way that F] [minimizes C] P] [[disruption P] [to F] [[the E] [project C] A] A] A]

Note that “disruption to the project” is a scene and is therefore analyzed in terms of a P and an A.

  1. [This means L]
  1. [you A] [should tell ] [people A] [ [you A2] [are leaving P] A]
  2. [and L]
  3. [ [ [ [take C-] [the proper E] [steps -C] E] [to F] [ensure C] E] P-] [that F] [others A] [ [ [can E] [pick up C] C] -P]

“take...steps” is the core of the secondary verb and therefore the center of “take the proper steps”. “the” and “proper” are not obligatory (one can say “take steps”) and they are used to refine “take steps”, they are therefore marked as E.

“pick up” should not be analyzed further, since it is a phrasal verb and therefore the verb and the preposition alone are not meaningful sub-parts in the meaning of the entire unit.

  1. [where L]
  2. [you A] [leave off P]

Example #3:

                “In the early 1680s, King Charles II of England began taking

steps to reorganize the colonies of New England. The charter of the Massachusetts Bay Colony was revoked in 1684 after its Puritan rulers refused to act on his demands for reforms in the colony, when Charles sought to streamline the administration of the small colonies and bring them more closely under crown control. He died in 1685 and his successor, the Roman Catholic James II, continued the process, which culminated in the creation of the Dominion of New England.” (Wikipedia, “1689 Boston Revolt”)

Delimiting Scenes:

  1. [In the early 1680s, King Charles II of England began taking  steps to  reorganize the colonies of New England. LU]

Note that LU is the default category for a scene, even if it is not linked in any way (see “Classification of Scenes” in “UCCA in a Nutshell”).

  1. [The charter of the Massachusetts Bay Colony was revoked in 1684 LU1]
  2. [after L<LU1,LU2>]

One could say that the “after” is an elaborator of the 1684. This is a reasonable analysis, but as we clearly have two independent scenes here, I would prefer the linkage analysis (a similar case is with (l) below).

  1. [its Puritan rulers refused to act on his demands for reforms in the colony LU2]

Refused is a secondary verb.

  1. [when L<LU2,LU3>]
  2. [Charles sought to streamline the administration of the small colonies LU3]
  3. [and L<LU3,LU4>]
  4. [bring them more closely under crown control LU4]
  5. [He died in 1685 LU5]
  6. [and L<LU5,LU6>]
  7. [his successor, the Roman Catholic James II, continued the process LU6]
  8. [which culminated in L<LU6,LU7>] (linking LU6 and LU7)
  9. [the creation of the Dominion of New England LU7]

About (l), there is a question of whether to refer to it as an elaborator of process, as  (which superficially resembles an elaborator). Both analyses are reasonable. The reason I chose the one stated is that it seems both of these units can stand on their own right, the “which” merely being means not to repeat the word “process” twice. I would imagine there are many cases where a similar meaning is conveyed by two autonomous scenes.

Identifying Scene Elements:

  1. [In the early 1680s D], [King Charles II of England A] [began taking steps to  reorganize P] [the colonies of New England A].

There is a question of whether this is two scenes or one. Since “began taking steps” refers to the same action as “regorganize” and merely used for hedging and for specification of the main verb, we analyze it as one scene with a secondary verb.

  1. [The charter of the Massachusetts Bay Colony A] [was revoked P] [in 1684 D]
  2. [after L<LU1,LU2>]
  1. [its Puritan rulers A] [refused to act P] [on his demands for reforms in the colony A]
  2. [when L<LU2,LU3>]
  3. [Charles A1] [sought to streamline P] [the administration of the small colonies A]
  4. [and L]
  5. [bring P] [them A] [more closely under crown control A] (remote A1)
  6. [He A] [died P] [in 1685 D]
  7. [and L]
  8. [his successor, the Roman Catholic James II A], [continued the process P]

Note that “continued the process” is one P, because “continue” is a secondary verb.

  1. [which culminated in L<LU6,LU7>] (linking LU6 and LU7)

“culminated” is a verb that links two scenes, but does not add much information besides expressing the relation between the two scenes (it does not evoke any new participants). It is therefore analyzed as an L.

Note that the “in” is an inherent preposition of “culminated” (i.e., necessitated by “culminated” but does not alter its meaning).

  1. [the E] [ [creation P] [of F] [the Dominion of New England A] C]

Note that  “the” acts as an elaborator of the entire scene.

Analyzing Complex Entities and Adding Referents:

  1. [[In R] [the E] [early E] [1680s C] D], [[King Charles II C] [of F] [England E] A] [ [began E] [[taking steps E] [to F] [reorganize C] C] ] [[the E] [colonies C] [of F] [New England E] A].

Note that “New England” here is an elaborator, since it specifies which colonies are concerned. It seems less reasonable in this case to think of “the colonies” as something which selects some part of New England.

Also note that “taking steps” is a fixed expression and is therefore not internally analyzed.

  1. [[The E] [charter C] [of F] [the Massachusetts Bay Colony E] A] [[was F] [revoked C] P] [in R] [1684 C] D]
  2. after L<LU1,LU2>]
  1. [[its E] [Puritan E] [rulers C] A] [[refused E] [to F] [act C] P] [[on F] [F [his A] [demands P] [[for R] [reforms C] [[in R] [the E] [colony C] A] A]

“its” refers to “the Massachusetts Bay Colony”. “his” refers to “King Charles II of England”. Note that “his demands for reforms in the colony” is a complete scene with a  and As.

  1. [when L<LU2,LU3>]
  2. [Charles A1] [[sought E] [to F] [streamline C] P] [[the E] [ [administration P] [of F] [[the E] [small E] [colonies C] A] C] A]

Here “sought” is a secondary verb, since it is something like “tried”. It does not correspond to any separate action or movement, but only modifies the primary verb “streamline”.

  1. [and L]
  2. [bring P] [them A] [ [ [[more E] [closely C] E] [under C] R] [crown E] [control C] A] (remote A1)

“more closely under” is a relatively infrequent construction, where the elaborator (“under”) is elaborated by another modifier (“closely”), which is in turn modified by another relation (“more”).

“them” refers to “the small colonies”.

  1. [He A] [died P] [[in R] [1685 C] D]

“he” again refers to “King Charles II of England”.

  1. [and L]
  2. [[[his E] [successor C] E], [the E] [Roman Catholic E] [James II C] A], [[continued E] [the process C] P]

Again, reference to “King Charles II of England”. Note that “his successor” refers to the same entity as “the Roman Catholic James II”. However, it seems reasonable to say that “his successor” elaborates “the Roman Catholic James II” and not the other way around, as the latter describes the participant much more elaborately.

  1. [which culminated in L]
  2. [the E] [ [creation P] [of F] [[the E] [Dominion E] [of F] [New England C] A] C]

“Dominion” here is a classifier (indicates the class of) “New England”. Therefore “New England” is the C, and “Dominion” is the E.

Example #4:

“The British Empire comprised the dominions, colonies, protectorates, mandates and other territories ruled or administered by the United Kingdom. It originated with the overseas colonies and trading posts established by England in the late 16th and early 17th centuries. At its height, it was the largest empire in history and, for over a century, was the foremost global power. By 1922 the British Empire held sway over about 458 million people, one-fifth of the world's population at the time, and covered more than 33,700,000 km^2 (13,012,000 sq mi), almost a quarter of the Earth's total land area. As a result, its political, linguistic and cultural legacy is widespread. At the peak of its power, it was often said that "the sun never sets on the British Empire" because its span across the globe ensured that the sun was always shining on at least one of its numerous territories.” (Wikipedia - “The British Empire”)

Delimiting Scenes:

  1. [The British Empire comprised the dominions, colonies, protectorates, mandates and other territories ruled or administered by the United Kingdom. LU]
  2. [It originated with the overseas colonies and trading posts established by England in the late 16th and early 17th centuries. LU]
  3. [At its height, it was the largest empire in history LU]
  4. [and, L]
  5. [for over a century, was the foremost global power. LU]
  6. [By 1922 the British Empire held sway over about 458 million people, one-fifth of the world's population at the time LU]
  7. [and L]
  8. [covered more than 33,700,000 km^2 (13,012,000 sq mi), almost a quarter of the Earth's total land area. LU]
  9. [As a result, L]
  10. [its political, linguistic and cultural legacy is widespread. LU]
  11. [At the peak of its power, it was often said that "the sun never sets on the British Empire" LU]

"the sun never sets on the British Empire" is a participant scene and therefore a part of the same unit as its P, namely “said”.

  1. [because L]
  2. [its span across the globe ensured that the sun was always shining on at least one of its numerous territories. LU]

“ensured” is a secondary verb and is therefore a forms a non-contiguous P as “was shining”.

Identifying Scene Elements:

  1. [The British Empire A1] [comprised S] [the dominions, colonies, protectorates, mandates and other territories ruled or administered by the United Kingdom A].
  2. [It A][a] [originated P] [with the overseas colonies and trading posts established by England in the late 16th and early 17th centuries A].
  3. [At its height D], [it A] [was S] [the largest empire in history A]
  4. [and, L]
  5. [for over a century D], [was S] [the foremost global power A]. (remote A: A1)

Note that it is equivalent to choose as a remote A the “it” in C, whose referent is A1.

  1. [By 1922, D] [the British Empire A2] [held sway P] [over about 458 million people, one-fifth of the world's population at the time A]

“over” is an inherent preposition of “hold sway”. See the internal analysis of this scene below.

  1. [and L]
  1. [covered S] [more than 33,700,000 km^2 (13,012,000 sq mi),almost a quarter of the Earth's total land area. A] (remote A: A2)

Quantities are seen in the current layer of UCCA as As.

  1. [As a result, L]
  2. [its political, linguistic and cultural legacy A] [is widespread S].
  3. [At the peak of its power D], [it F] [was often said P] [that F] [ "[the sun A] [never D] [sets P] [on the British Empire A]" A]
  1. [because L]
  2. [its span across the globe A] [ensured P-] [that F] [the sun A] [was -P-] [always D] [shining -P] [on at least one of its numerous territories A].

Note that “ensure” is a secondary verb that introduces another role (whose filler in this case is “its span across the globe”).

Analyzing Complex Entities and Adding Referents:

  1. [The British Empire A] [comprised S] [ [the E] [ [dominions C], [colonies C], [protectorates C], [mandates C] [and N] [[other E] [territories C] C] C3] [ [ [ruled C] [or N] [administered C] P] [ [by F] [the E] [United Kingdom C] A] E{C3}] A].

Note that the elaborator scene has a remote A (C3).

Note that we have a complex P here “ruled or administered”. Another possible annotation would have been to separate it into two annotations. However, there are two reasons not to do so here:

  1. The two predicates share their participants, their ground (speech event), their time and location.
  2. The two predicates are construed as referring to the same one action (but perhaps different aspects of it).

Both “British Empire” and “United Kingdom” are considered unanalyzable since they are usually used as fixed expressions for referring to specific entities.

  1. [It A] [originated P] [ [with R] [the E] [overseas E] [ [colonies C] [and N] [trading posts C] C] [ [established P] [ [by F] [England C] A] [ [in E] [the E] [ [[late E] [16th C] C8] [and N] [ [early E] [17th C] C9] E] [centuries C] E] E] A].

The expression “in the late 16th and early 17th centuries” demands some explanation. The analysis given here assumes “centuries” evokes a streamline of the centuries, of which both “late 16th” and “early 17th” select a part. Another reasonable analysis here is to say we have a coordination of two Es (in the late 16th century and in the early 17th century) and that there are remote units in them. This is a reasonable analysis, but it is substantially more complicated.

  1. [ [At E] [its E] [height C] D], [it A] [was S] [ [the E] [largest E] [empire C] [[in R] [history C] E] A]

“At its height” is a D, since it is a temporal modifier. The “was” here is an S  (and not and F) since it expresses an identity relation.

  1. [and, N]
  2. [ [for E] [over E] [a E] [century C] E], [was S] [ [the E] [foremost E] [global E] [power C] A]. (remote A: A1)
  3. [ [By E] [1922 C], D] [the British Empire A2] [held sway P] [ [over F] [about E] [ [458 million E] [people C] C], [ [one-fifth E] [of F] [the E] [world E] ['s F] [population C] [ [at the time E] E] A]

“held sway” is one process (it doesn’t make such sense to say there is some other relation expressed here between “held” and “sway”). “over” is an inherent preposition of “held sway” and therefore an F. “at the time” is a fixed expression.

  1. [and L]
  1. [covered S] [ [ [more E] [than F] [33,700,000 km^2 C] [(13,012,000 sq mi) E], [[almost E] [a E] [quarter C] [of F] [ [the E] [Earth E] ['s F] [total E] [land E] [area C] E] A] . (remote A2)

“than” is an F. The main semantic relation is delivered by more (which is therefore an E). “a” is considered an E since it reflects that it’s one quarter (and not two or three) and also insinuates it is no special quarter (rather an indefinite one).

  1. [As a result, L]

This is a fixed expression, without really any internal structure.

  1. [ [its E] [ [political C], [linguistic C] [and L] [cultural C] E] [legacy A] [is widespread S].
  2. [[At E] [ [the E] [peak C] E] [of F] [its E] [power C] D], [it F] [ [was F] P-] [often D] [[said C] -P] [that F] [ [ [the E] [sun C] A] [never D] [sets P] [ [on R] [the British Empire C] A] A]
